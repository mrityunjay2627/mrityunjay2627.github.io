I started out at VIT with a B.Tech. in CS & Business Systems — a hybrid program that paired the engineering core with finance, operations, and product thinking. That mix turned out to be the thread that's followed me everywhere since.

My first real exposure to the field was a six-month stint as a business analyst at Upthrust, where I lived in BI dashboards, built sales funnels with senior leadership, and learned how a clean piece of analysis can move a real decision. The summer after that, I joined Eigengram — a medical AI startup — as a software engineer intern, working alongside the two technical founders on data pipelines and model deployment for clinical applications. It was my first taste of what shipping AI under real constraints actually feels like.

From there I joined Deloitte as a Consulting Analyst in Strategy & Analytics, where I spent a year embedded with global clients — building AI systems on tight deadlines, presenting architecture tradeoffs to senior stakeholders, and operating inside an agile delivery framework with code reviews, sprint planning, and weekly client demos. That's where I learned to translate technical complexity into business impact without losing the underlying rigor.

I'm now finishing an M.S. in Robotics & AI at Arizona State University with a 3.89 GPA. Beyond the core AI/ML coursework, I've worked on symbolic planning, reinforcement learning, and digital twins for collaborative robots — wiring perception, kinematics, and learning into systems that move actual hardware. Two research roles along the way: agentic AI for aerospace mission analysis at the CoDe Lab, and autonomous blimp control at the AMASS Lab.

What I bring to a team is range. I move fluidly between Python data pipelines, cloud infrastructure, LLM orchestration frameworks, and containerized deployments — but the real value isn't tool fluency. It's knowing when Kafka is overkill versus when Kinesis won't scale, or why a RAG pipeline fails without proper chunking.

On the ML side, that same instinct shows up around when LoRA is enough versus when you actually need a full fine-tune, when a quick CNN beats reaching for a Vision Transformer, and how to read a training curve before the metrics catch up to you. And on the robotics software side, it's knowing when symbolic planning gives you the guarantees RL won't, when a digital twin saves a week of hardware iteration, and where coordinate frames usually go wrong.

I'm looking for roles where I'm embedded with the problem, not isolated in a backend silo — building systems that ship, learning the user's domain, and getting energized by messy, high-impact work.
